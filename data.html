Sub-Question 2

The fake review problem
is bigger than most people realise.

Fake reviews don't just mislead individual consumers - they erode trust in entire platforms. When trust falls below a threshold, consumers stop acting on any reviews, real or fabricated (Pena-Garcia et al., 2024; Filieri et al., 2015).

How to read this section

The numbers are platform-reported, but their meaning depends on context. A removal count measures detection and moderation activity, not the true total number of fake reviews that existed.

Step 01

Separate removals from prevalence

2.7M removals show scale, but not the full size of the hidden fake-review market.

Step 02

Separate AI text from all fraud

214K suspected AI-generated removals are one category within a wider fraud ecosystem.

Step 03

Connect the numbers to trust

Consumers do not only judge individual reviews. They judge whether the platform itself feels reliable.

Research Synthesis

The proliferation of AI-generated reviews is not merely a volume problem - it is a trust infrastructure problem. Wu et al. (2020) found detection approaches remain difficult to scale reliably. Pena-Garcia et al. (2024) confirmed that when consumers detect fakeness, trust can decline not only in individual reviews but also in the rating systems hosting them. The 137% increase documented by Originality.AI (2024) suggests this risk may increase.

Sub-Question 3

How a hotel responds matters
as much as the review itself.

Management responses have independent effects on consumer trust - over and above the original review content. Timing, tone, and specificity all matter (Sparks & Browning, 2011; Sparks et al., 2016).

Ineffective Response

"We're sorry you had a bad experience. We hope to see you again soon."

Generic. Doesn't acknowledge the specific complaint. Signals indifference. Consumers read this and trust the negative review more - not less (Sparks & Browning, 2011).

Effective Response

"Hi Sarah - thank you for flagging the noise issue on Floor 6. We've moved those guests and added soundproofing. Book direct and we'll ensure a quieter room."

Specific, personal, shows accountability, offers a remedy. Prospective guests see a property that listens and acts - partially offsetting even severe negative reviews (Sparks et al., 2016).

Domain 1

Rating gap across platforms (G)

What it means: how far apart the same hotel’s ratings are across platforms after converting them to the same 5-point scale.

  • Tripadvisor and Google are already usually out of 5.
  • Booking.com is out of 10, so divide by 2 to convert it to a 5-point scale.
  • Formula: rating gap = highest converted rating - lowest converted rating.

Tripadvisor-Google example: Tripadvisor = 4.1 / 5 and Google = 4.3 / 5. Rating gap = 4.3 - 4.1 = 0.2 stars.

Domain 2

Fake or AI review risk (F)

What it means: a quick judgement of whether the review profile shows warning signs of manipulation, review boosting, sabotage, or AI-generated language.

  • 0-25% low risk: specific, varied, recent reviews from different reviewer histories.
  • 26-60% moderate risk: some vague praise, repeated phrases, sudden clusters, or uneven reviewer histories.
  • 61-100% high risk: many reviews look templated, overly polished, posted in bursts, or lack details about rooms, staff, dates, or incidents.
Domain 3

Review volume strength (V)

What it means: how much confidence the total review volume gives you. More recent and diverse reviews make one suspicious review less influential.

  • 0-30% weak: few reviews, old reviews, or only one platform has meaningful volume.
  • 31-70% moderate: enough reviews to see patterns, but volume is uneven or not recent.
  • 71-100% strong: many recent reviews across multiple platforms with consistent patterns.
Domain 4

Management response quality (M)

What it means: how well the hotel responds to reviews, especially negative ones. Judge specificity, tone, and accountability.

  • 0-30% weak: no response, defensive tone, or generic copy-paste apology.
  • 31-70% moderate: polite response, but limited detail or unclear corrective action.
  • 71-100% strong: names the issue, thanks the guest, explains what changed, and invites follow-up appropriately.

Worked example: how the sliders translate into a trust score

Step 1, rating gap: Tripadvisor = 4.1 / 5 and Google = 4.3 / 5, so the gap is 4.3 - 4.1 = 0.2 stars. If Booking.com is included, convert 8.2 / 10 to 4.1 / 5 first, then compare all converted scores.

Step 2, fake or AI risk: if reviews are detailed and varied, choose about 20-30%. If many reviews are vague, repetitive, overly polished, or posted in bursts, choose 60-80%.

Step 3, review volume: if there are only a few reviews, choose 20-30%. If the hotel has many recent reviews across several platforms, choose 75-90%.

Step 4, management response quality: if responses are generic, choose 20-30%. If they name the problem and describe action taken, choose 75-90%.

Example score: gap = 0.2, fake/AI risk = 30%, review volume = 80%, and response quality = 85%. Formula: 100 - 18(0.2) - 0.30(30) + 0.10(80) + 0.20(85) = 112.4, capped at 100. This profile would be interpreted as high trust.
