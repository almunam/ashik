About This Project

Why This Research Question?

Working at a hotel front desk in Niagara Falls made one thing clear: guests arrive with their phones open, screenshots of three different platforms showing three different scores for the same property. The confusion is real, and so are its consequences for booking decisions.

This capstone project examines how structural differences between TripAdvisor, Booking.com, and Google Reviews produce inconsistent ratings, how AI-generated and fraudulent content amplifies distrust, and how hotel management communication either restores or erodes consumer confidence. The research draws on a systematic review of peer-reviewed scholarship and secondary analysis of publicly available review data.

Key sources include Kirilenko et al. (2024), Xiang et al. (2017), Sparks and Browning (2011), and Wu et al. (2020).

Project Scope

Focused on hotel accommodation on TripAdvisor, Booking.com, and Google Reviews. Literature from 2015 to 2025. Secondary content analysis draws on publicly available review data for five Niagara Falls properties and on previously published platform-level analyses. No original data collection or participant recruitment.

Three Research Themes

Click each theme to expand the literature context.

Xiang et al. (2017) showed that TripAdvisor, Expedia, and Yelp produce systematically different ratings for the same Manhattan hotels. Building on this, Kirilenko et al. (2024) demonstrated that the divergence is not static but temporal: as platforms update their algorithms and review volume grows, the gap between open and closed platforms widens over time. Where Xiang et al. treat divergence as primarily structural, Kirilenko et al. reframe it as dynamic, suggesting consumer trust is not just a platform issue but a moving target for hoteliers. The global communication dimension is significant: what a five-star rating signals on a North American Google Reviews profile may carry a different interpretive weight than the same score on a European Booking.com page, as rating culture and platform norms vary across markets.

Wu et al. (2020) documented the full range of fake review mechanisms, from incentivised posting to automated generation, and found that no existing detection approach provides reliable, scalable protection. Hajek et al. (2020) extended this at the technical level, showing that rating deviations are among the strongest computational signals of fraud. Taken together, these studies reveal a tension: platforms have detection tools, but those tools are consistently outpaced by new generation methods. Pena-Garcia et al. (2024) add a consumer-side dimension: suspicion of fake reviews degrades trust not just for the flagged item but across the platform as a whole, producing a spillover effect that Filieri et al. (2015) would explain through platform trustworthiness as an antecedent to review adoption.

Sparks and Browning (2011) established that management responses shift consumer perceptions even when the underlying review content is unchanged. Sparks et al. (2016) refined this: specificity matters more than tone, with acknowledgement of the exact concern raised being the strongest predictor of trust restoration. However, Ladhari and Michaud (2015) introduce a complication: source credibility mediates how much weight a response carries, which means the same management reply will land differently on a closed platform like Booking.com than on an open one like TripAdvisor. This cross-platform variability in response effectiveness is a gap the existing literature has not yet fully addressed.

Theme 02

The Authenticity Crisis

The credibility of online reviews rests on one assumption: that they reflect genuine consumer experiences. When that assumption fails, the information value of the entire ecosystem degrades (Wu et al., 2020).

Hajek et al. (2020) confirmed that rating deviations are among the most reliable computational signals of fraudulent content. However, where Hajek et al. focus on detection from the platform side, Pena-Garcia et al. (2024) shift the lens to the consumer side, finding that suspicion of fake reviews produces a spillover: trust degrades not just for the flagged item but across the entire platform. Filieri et al. (2015) provide the theoretical anchor, showing that platform trustworthiness is a prerequisite for review adoption. Together, these three studies reveal a compounding dynamic: fake reviews undermine platform trust, which in turn reduces the utility of all reviews, authentic or not.

As generative AI tools become more sophisticated, the surface features that once distinguished machine-produced text from human-produced text are becoming less reliable markers, further straining the user's interpretive framework.

Literature Synthesis

"While Wu et al. (2020) document the mechanisms of fake review production, Pena-Garcia et al. (2024) reveal the downstream consequence: it is not just the fraudulent review that consumers distrust, but the platform that hosts it. Filieri et al. (2015) complete the picture by showing that once platform trustworthiness falls below a threshold, the review adoption process breaks down entirely."

Theme 03

How Hotels Respond and Why It Matters

The core finding in the management response literature is that how a business responds to reviews has independent effects on consumer trust and booking intention, over and above the content of the original review (Chen and Law, 2016; Sparks and Browning, 2011).

Sparks et al. (2016) refined this: responses that acknowledge specific concerns and offer concrete remedies outperform generic apologies significantly. The platform context shapes these dynamics: on Booking.com, management responses tied to verified transactions carry more weight than on TripAdvisor (Kirilenko et al., 2024). Active engagement on all platforms was found to enhance booking intentions among prospective guests (Elmedulan et al., 2025).

Author Synthesis

While Ladhari and Michaud (2015) show that eWOM source credibility mediates the effect of reviews on trust, Pena-Garcia et al. (2024) extend this by demonstrating that the platform itself is a credibility signal. This places management responses in a more complex context than Sparks and Browning (2011) originally assumed: the same reply carries different interpretive weight depending on whether the platform verifies the underlying review. Hoteliers cannot treat response strategy as platform-neutral.

Effective

Acknowledge the Specific Concern

Responses that name the exact issue raised are significantly more effective at restoring trust than generic apologies. Specificity is the strongest predictor of positive consumer reappraisal (Sparks and Browning, 2011; Sparks et al., 2016).

Ineffective

Defensive or Dismissive Replies

Responses that challenge the guest's account or redirect blame can actively worsen consumer perceptions. An absent response is often preferable to a defensive one (Sparks et al., 2016).

Platform Context

Responses Land Differently by Platform

On Booking.com, responses tied to verified stays carry more credibility weight. On TripAdvisor, where the underlying review may be unverified, consumers apply more scepticism to both the review and the response (Kirilenko et al., 2024).

Strategic Insight

Consistency Across Platforms Is Essential

Guests who consult multiple platforms before booking notice inconsistencies in management communication. Active engagement across all platforms enhanced booking intentions (Elmedulan et al., 2025; Ladhari and Michaud, 2015).

Practical Takeaways

Recommendations for Hospitality Professionals

Click each item to read the evidence behind it. Synthesised from the scholarly literature reviewed in this capstone.

01

Monitor All Three Platforms Simultaneously

Rating discrepancies between TripAdvisor, Booking.com, and Google Reviews are structural and predictable (Kirilenko et al., 2024; Xiang et al., 2017). Relying on a single platform as a proxy for reputation risks missing divergence that prospective guests will see when they cross-check before booking.

02

Respond Consistently and Specifically

Target a response rate above 80% on all platforms. Generic responses do not produce the trust-restoration effect documented by Sparks and Browning (2011). Only responses that name and address the actual concern have a measurable positive effect.

03

Flag Suspicious Review Patterns Proactively

Sudden spikes in review volume, clusters of similar language, and extreme bimodal rating patterns are documented signals of manipulation (Hajek et al., 2020; Wu et al., 2020). The Coalition for Trusted Reviews, formed by TripAdvisor, Booking.com, and others in 2023, provides additional reporting channels (TripAdvisor, 2025).

04

Encourage Verified Post-Stay Reviews

On open platforms, authentic verified reviews help dilute the influence of fraudulent content and bring rating distributions closer to the moderated patterns seen on closed platforms (Kirilenko et al., 2024). Simple post-stay follow-ups via email consistently increase authentic review volume.

05

Align Digital Communication with Service Reality

Digital reputation management complements but cannot replace service quality. Once consumers perceive inauthenticity at the platform level, even genuine positive reviews are discounted (Pena-Garcia et al., 2024; Filieri et al., 2015).
