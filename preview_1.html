Platform Structure & Rating Divergence

Why the same hotel earns different scores on TripAdvisor, Booking.com, and Google Reviews — and what that means for consumer trust.

Primary sources: Xiang et al. (2017) · Kirilenko et al. (2024) · TripAdvisor Transparency Report (2025)

This infographic uses high-contrast colours, semantic headings, and text alternatives for all visual elements. All data is also presented in text form.
⚠

Key Finding: The same hotel can receive meaningfully different star ratings across platforms — not because the hotel changed, but because each platform's architecture, audience, and verification model shapes the reviews it receives (Kirilenko et al., 2024).

Addresses Sub-Question 1 — Structural & Algorithmic Factors

How Each Platform Works

Open Platform

TripAdvisor

Rating scale: 1–5 bubbles

Any registered user may submit a review regardless of whether a stay was verified. Drives high review volume and breadth, but creates significant vulnerability to manipulation and extreme-rating bias.

Review Access Any registered user — no booking verification required
Rating Distribution Bimodal — extreme scores (1★ and 5★) overrepresented (Kirilenko et al., 2024)
Fake Review Removal (2024) 2.7 million total; 214,000 AI-generated (TripAdvisor, 2025)
Review Boosting Accounts for 54% of all removals (TripAdvisor, 2025)
Closed Platform

Booking.com

Rating scale: 1–10 numeric

Reviews restricted to guests who completed a verified booking through the platform. This adds a verification layer that moderates rating distributions and reduces — but does not eliminate — fake content.

Review Access Verified booking through Booking.com required
Rating Distribution More moderate — fewer extreme scores due to verification barrier
Management Response Appears directly beneath verified review — carries additional credibility weight (Kirilenko et al., 2024)
Semi-Open Platform

Google Reviews

Rating scale: 1–5 stars

Accessible to any Google account holder, loosely tied to location check-ins and local search behaviour. Sits between open and closed models in both verification stringency and rating patterns.

Review Access Any Google account holder — loose location check-in tie
Rating Distribution Intermediate — between TripAdvisor and Booking.com patterns
Integration Tied to local search; high visibility for non-travel-specific consumers

Rating Distribution Patterns by Platform Type

Open Platforms

e.g. TripAdvisor

1★2★3★4★5★

Bimodal distribution. Extreme ratings (1★ and 5★) are overrepresented. Highly motivated reviewers — those with strong positive or negative experiences — disproportionately shape the overall picture. Over time, a U-shape emerges (Kirilenko et al., 2024).

Closed Platforms

e.g. Booking.com

1★2★3★4★5★

More moderate distribution. Verification requirement filters out the least engaged and most adversarial reviewers. Ratings cluster more toward the middle-to-upper range. Platform credibility is higher but volume is lower (Kirilenko et al., 2024).

Research Synthesis

Treating review data from different platforms as equivalent is methodologically and practically unjustified (Xiang et al., 2017). While Xiang et al. established that platforms differ in linguistic characteristics, sentiment, and topic focus, Kirilenko et al. (2024) extended this finding temporally — showing that divergence is not static but shifts as platforms update their algorithms. For consumers navigating multiple platforms simultaneously, this creates an interpretive challenge that can erode trust in the review process itself, not just in individual properties.
